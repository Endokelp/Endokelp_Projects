The history of portfolio selection as a formal mathematical problem begins in 1952 with Harry Markowitz. Before the publication of 'Portfolio Selection,' the academic understanding of investment was fragmented and largely focused on the micro-level characteristics of secular assets. The prevailing wisdom was simply to 'buy good companies' and hold them for the long term. Markowitz’s revolutionary contribution was the application of quadratic programming and statistical variance to the problem of capital deployment.

Markowitz introduced the concept of the 'Efficient Frontier,' a parabolic curve that represents the set of portfolios that provide the maximum expected return for a given level of risk (measured as standard deviation). Within this framework, the investor's goal is to move as far to the upper-left of this frontier as possible. However, the true genius of Markowitz’s work lay in his recognition that diversification is not about the number of assets held, but about the correlation between those assets. A portfolio of ten tech stocks is far riskier than a portfolio composed of one tech stock, one gold bar, and one treasury bond, even though the latter has fewer components.

Following in Markowitz's wake, the 1960s saw the development of the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) by William Sharpe, John Lintner, and Jan Mossin. CAPM simplified the optimization problem by introducing the concept of the 'Market Portfolio.' According to CAPM, all investors should hold a combination of the risk-free asset and the market portfolio, with the proportion determined by their individual risk tolerance. This gave birth to the 'Capital Market Line' (CML), a straight line extending from the risk-free rate and tangency point on the Efficient Frontier. Sharpe’s eponymous Ratio (Return minus Risk-Free Rate divided by Standard Deviation) became the standard by which all subsequent money managers would be judged.

Parallel to these developments, Eugene Fama formalized the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH). Fama's central thesis was that in a market with rational actors and perfect information, asset prices always reflect all available data. If prices are 'correct' at all times, then over-performance (Alpha) is impossible to achieve consistently, and the best strategy for any investor is to hold a broad index. This led to a multi-decade debate between the 'Active Management' school (who believed in market inefficiencies) and the 'Passive Management' school (who favored low-cost indexing).

However, as the 20th century closed, these classical theories began to face intense scrutiny. The failure of the hedge fund Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM) in 1998, despite being led by Nobel laureates, exposed the 'Model Risk' inherent in quantitative finance. The quants had assumed that the risk of a Russian debt default was a '6-sigma' event—essentially impossible according to their normal distributions. When it happened, their portfolios collapsed, revealing that the 'Fat Tails' of reality were far more dangerous than the smooth curves of theory.

In the contemporary era, the work of Marcos Lopez de Prado has led to a paradigm shift in how we view diversification. Lopez de Prado argued that the traditional Markowitz covariance matrix is often numerically unstable. Because asset returns are often highly correlated, the matrix can become 'ill-conditioned,' meaning that even a tiny change in input (such as one day's return) can result in a massive change in the 'optimal' weights. To solve this, he developed Hierarchical Risk Parity (HRP).

HRP moves away from the global optimization of the entire matrix and instead utilizes machine learning techniques—specifically hierarchical clustering—to understand the relationships between assets. By grouping assets into clusters and then allocating risk between those clusters, HRP avoids the need for matrix inversion altogether. This approach is particularly effective in modern markets where 'regime shifts' can cause classical correlations to fail.

The literature review for this dissertation thus spans from the deterministic optimism of the 1950s to the probabilistic realism of the 2020s. We see a clear trajectory: from the belief in static, efficient markets to the recognition that markets are complex, adaptive systems that require equally adaptive algorithmic management. This study sits at the intersection of these two worlds, testing if the modern clustering of de Prado can indeed overcome the historical instabilities of Markowitz.
