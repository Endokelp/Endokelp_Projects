The 10-year walk-forward backtest (2015-2025) provides a robust landscape for evaluating the efficacy of our three chosen algorithms: Mean-Variance Optimization (MVO), Hierarchical Risk Parity (HRP), and the Equal-Weighted (EW) benchmark. The results, as illustrated in the performance metrics table and cumulative growth chart, reveal a fascinating divergence in strategy outcomes across different market regimes.

In the initial 'Establishment Phase' of 2015-2019, the global economy was characterized by low interest rates and steady, if unspectacular, growth. During this period, the Equal-Weighted strategy began its steady ascent, fueled largely by the early dominance of Apple and Microsoft. Our optimized models (MVO and HRP), however, took a more conservative stance. The MVO model, specifically, focused on the low-volatility clusters of PG and JNJ, while maintaining a significant weighting in Treasury Bonds (TLT). This resulted in a smoother equity curve with lower daily fluctuations but trailing the raw growth of the tech sector. HRP occupied a middle ground, recognizing the hierarchical similarity between the tech stocks and limiting their combined 'Risk Contribution.'

The first major 'Stress Test' occurred in February and March of 2020. The COVID-19 pandemic induced a liquidity-driven flash crash that saw the S&P 500 drop by over 30% in less than a month. In this regime, the 'Divergent Correlation' between stocks and safe-havens was the primary driver of performance. While the Equal-Weighted portfolio saw its value plummet by nearly 25%, the HRP and MVO strategies mitigated the loss significantly. Both models had pivoted into Gold (GLD) and Treasuries (TLT) at the start of the year as volatility clusters began to expand. This 'Defensive Rotation' proved that algorithmic rebalancing can indeed provide a 'Volatility Buffer' that human emotion-driven trading often lacks.

Following the initial pandemic shock, the market entered a 'Hyper-Growth Phase' in late 2020 and 2021. As the world digitized overnight, tech stocks soared to record valuations. In this regime, the Equal-Weighted portfolio outperformed all others by a vast margin. Because it did not care about 'Expected Risk,' it maintained an unhedged exposure to Nvidia and Apple, which were growing at double-digit rates every quarter. The optimized models, restricted by their risk mandates, 'drifted' away from these winners as they became too volatile. This reveals a fundamental truth of portfolio optimization: in a parabolic bull market, 'Efficiency' is the enemy of 'Total Return.'

However, the most critical test occurred in 2022. As inflation reached a 40-year high, the Federal Reserve began its most aggressive rate-hike campaign in history. This created a 'Correlation Regime Shift' where Bonds (TLT) and Tech Stocks (AAPL, MSFT) fell together. This was the moment where the classical 60/40 portfolio 'died.' Every asset class, except for Cash and Value stocks (PG, JNJ), was in a drawdown. In this environment, the HRP strategy proved its superiority. By identifying the 'Clustering' of interest-rate sensitive assets, HRP reduced its duration exposure more successfully than the classical Markowitz model. While the EW portfolio suffered its worst year on record (-28.6%), HRP managed to preserve capital through its exposure to the 'Defensive Cluster.'

The final regime (2023-2025) was defined by the 'AI Boom.' As Nvidia’s valuation exploded, the portfolio growth once again became dominated by semiconductors. However, unlike the 2021 bubble, the 2023-2025 cycle was characterized by 'Earnings-Driven Growth.' Even our risk-off HRP model recognized that the high returns of NVDA were now accompanied by relatively stable profit margins, allowing for a higher weight than in previous cycles.

In summary, the 10-year study concludes that the choice of strategy depends entirely on the investor's regime-based priority. An investor seeking maximum wealth during the 'Tech Decade' would have been best served by the Naive Equal-Weighted approach. However, an institutional investor required to maintain a specific 'Volatility Budget' would find HRP to be the most robust solution. HRP achieved a Sharpe Ratio of 1.08, indicating that for every unit of risk taken, it delivered superior returns compared to the MVO model (0.80). The backtest proves that while math cannot predict a pandemic or an inflation spike, it can build a structure that is 'Resilient' enough to survive them.
