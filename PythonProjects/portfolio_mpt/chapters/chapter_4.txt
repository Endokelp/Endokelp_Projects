The validity of any quantitative research is fundamentally tethered to the quality and scope of the underlying data. For this dissertation, we have meticulously constructed a multi-asset universe designed to represent the primary 'Regime Blocks' of the global economy over a decade-long horizon. The observation period, spanning from January 1, 2015, to January 1, 2025, encompasses 2,516 trading days—a sample size sufficient for robust statistical inference and the modeling of multiple market cycles.

Our asset selection strategy was governed by the need for 'Structural Diversification.' We avoided the pitfall of selecting only correlated equities, instead choosing eight pillars of the financial system:

1. The Technological Growth Engines (AAPL, MSFT, NVDA): These assets represent the frontier of the 'Silicon Economy.' Nvidia, specifically, provides a unique data point as it successfully transitioned from a high-volatility semiconductor cyclical into the world’s most valuable enterprise, driven by the Artificial Intelligence epoch. These assets provide the 'Beta' growth for our study but also introduce massive systemic correlation risk.

2. The Defensive Value Pillars (PG, JNJ): Procter & Gamble and Johnson & Johnson serve as the 'Low Beta' anchors of the portfolio. Because their revenues are derived from consumer staples and healthcare—sectors with inelastic demand—they typically exhibit a low correlation to the technical growth cycle, providing essential stability during market corrections.

3. The Fiat Hedge (GLD): Gold is a unique asset class that bears no yield and carries no credit risk. Historically, it has served as a safe haven during periods of currency devaluation and geopolitical uncertainty. By including physical gold exposure (via the GLD ETF), we introduce an asset that often decouples from equity and bond markets during systemic 'Risk-Off' events.

4. The Duration Hedge (TLT): Long-term 20+ Year Treasury Bonds represent the primary vehicle for interest-rate risk exposure. In a traditional 60/40 portfolio, bonds were expected to rise when stocks fell. However, our study analyzes the breakdown of this relationship in 2022, providing a critical test for our optimization models.

5. The Benchmark (SPY): The S&P 500 ETF serves as our baseline. It represents the 'Naive Market Return.' Any optimization strategy must prove its worth by delivering superior risk-adjusted returns (Sharpe Ratio) compared to this diversified but unmanaged index.

The data procurement process involved the retrieval of daily adjusted closing prices via the Yahoo Finance API (Open-Source Library yfinance). Adjusted prices are paramount for a long-term study, as they account for corporate actions such as the 10-for-1 Nvidia split in June 2024 and the 4-for-1 Apple split in August 2020. Without these adjustments, the return series would be corrupted by artificial price drops, leading to erroneous volatility calculations.

Once the raw prices were retrieved, they underwent a rigorous cleaning and transformation process. We used logarithmic returns rather than simple arithmetic returns. Log returns (ln(Pt / Pt-1)) are preferred for quantitative modeling for two primary reasons: first, they are additive across time, meaning the return over a month is simply the sum of the daily log returns; and second, they more closely approximate a normal distribution, facilitating the application of classical statistical tests. 

Following transformation, we calculated the annualized summary statistics: the mean return (μ), the standard deviation (σ), and the full covariance matrix (Σ). For the purposes of this study, we assumed a risk-free rate of 2.0%, which reflects the historical decade-average for the 10-Year Treasury Yield. This rate is used to calculate the Sharpe Ratio, our primary metric for evaluating the 'Efficiency' of the portfolios.

Finally, we implemented a 'Walk-Forward' methodology for our backtest. Instead of performing a single optimization at the start of 2015, our engine rebalances the portfolios every month. At each rebalancing date, the algorithms (MVO and HRP) are retrained on the previous 252 days (one year) of data. This simulates the real-world experience of a fund manager who must adapt to changing correlations and return expectations in real-time. This methodology ensures that our results are not the product of 'Look-ahead Bias' but a genuine reflection of how these algorithms handle the evolving noise and signals of the global market.
